# Combat: balance, equilibrium, afflictions, defences

Achaea's combat is governed by two recovering resources — **balance** and **equilibrium** — and by a continuously evolving set of **afflictions** and **defences**. Mastering combat is mastering the interaction between these four things.

## Balance vs equilibrium

Two resources, each independently consumed and recovered:

- **Balance** — physical balance. Consumed by physical actions (swinging a sword, drawing back a bow, throwing a knife).
- **Equilibrium** — mental composure. Consumed by mental and magical actions (casting a spell, focusing a power, performing telepathy).

Most offensive abilities require **both** balance and equilibrium. Using one consumes it for a fixed number of seconds — typically 2–5 — during which any ability that needs that resource is blocked. A swing of a heavy weapon might cost 4 seconds of balance; a quick jab might cost 1.8. Recovery is automatic and timed; there's no resource to spend to recover faster.

The asymmetry between physical and mental cooldowns is the lever for combat strategy: weave physical attacks (off balance only) with magical ones (off equilibrium only) so that one is always available.

## Healing has its own "balances"

Healing isn't free, either. Each healing method has its own cooldown that mimics balance/equilibrium:

- **Salves** — apply one salve at a time, with a salve-balance cooldown.
- **Herbs** — eat one herb at a time, with an herb-balance cooldown.
- **Sips** — drink one healing draught at a time, with a sip-balance cooldown.

A skilled player layers these — sip mana, eat a herb for curing, apply a salve to a wound — so that no single source of healing is the bottleneck.

## Afflictions

An **affliction** is a negative status effect: a wound, a poison, a hex, a broken limb, a mental confusion, a bleeding lung. Combat in Achaea is largely about stacking afflictions faster than the opponent can cure them. Each affliction has:

- A name (`asthma`, `slickness`, `paralysis`, `hellsight`, ...).
- One or more cures (a specific salve, herb, focus, or other action).
- An effect (blocks certain abilities, applies damage over time, prevents certain cures).

Afflictions stream over GMCP via `Char.Afflictions.{List,Add,Remove}` — nogfx decodes these in `pkg/gmcp/charafflictions.go`.

## Defences

A **defence** is a positive status effect: a ward, a buff, a passive resistance. Players prepare defences before combat — these consume balance/equilibrium and resources up front, and then persist until something strips them. Combat involves both maintaining your own defences and stripping the opponent's.

Defences stream via `Char.Defences.{List,Add,Remove}`; decoded in `pkg/gmcp/chardefences.go`.

## Vitals

Three primary vitals govern survival:

- **Health (HP)** — physical durability. Hit zero and you die.
- **Mana (MP)** — magical reserve. Spent by spells; needed for many class abilities.
- **Endurance** and **Willpower** — depleted by sustained activity, regenerated by rest.

Vitals stream via `Char.Vitals` (and Achaea-extended `Char.Status`). nogfx surfaces them through `pkg.CharacterVital` and renders them in the TUI vitals pane.

## Targeting

The current target is tracked via `IRE.Target.{Set,Info}`. nogfx maintains a `Target` struct (`pkg.Target` and `pkg/world/achaea/target.go`) with the concepts of:

- **Current target** — the name the server has accepted.
- **Candidates** — patterns the player has expressed interest in (e.g. specific NPC types).
- **Present** — entities currently in the room, used for autotargeting against candidates.

Setting a target sends an `IRE.Target.Set` upstream; the engine waits for the server's `IRE.Target.Info` confirmation before treating the change as official.

## Class differences

Different classes prioritise the balance / equilibrium / affliction / defence dimensions differently. A Monk leans on physical (balance) attacks with elaborate stance-based timing; an Apostate stacks afflictions through mental (equilibrium) magic; a Serpent layers slow venom afflictions that fire long after the attack. The shared resource model is what lets these wildly different toolkits be balanced against each other.
